What is Color Theory?

Color theory is the study of how colors interact with each other and how they affect our perception.

Key Areas of Study:

  • Color Relationships: How colors work together
  • Color Harmony: Creating visually pleasing combinations
  • Psychological Impact: How colors affect emotions and behavior

Color Classifications

Primary colors are the fundamental hues from which all other colors are derived.

Tertiary colors result from combining a primary color with a neighboring secondary color.

Yellow-Green
Blue-Green

Color Temperature

Warm Colors

Reds, oranges, and yellows evoke feelings of comfort, warmth, and coziness.

Cool Colors

Blues, greens, and purples evoke feelings of calmness, serenity, and professionalism.

Color Models

Color models are essential for describing and reproducing colors in a standard way.

  • RGB Model: Red, Green, Blue - Used for digital displays
  • HSV Model: Hue, Saturation, Value - Intuitive for color selection
  • HSL Model: Hue, Saturation, Lightness - Similar to HSV with different lightness calculation

These models represent colors based on different properties and will be explored in future lessons.

The Color Wheel

The color wheel is a circular diagram that shows how colors relate to each other. It's an essential tool for designers.

Traditional 12-color wheel showing color relationships

Purpose:

  • Helps select color combinations
  • Creates effective color palettes
  • Develops color schemes based on color theory principles

Color Schemes

A color scheme is the set of colors chosen for a specific design or project, based on principles of color theory.

By understanding the relationships between colors on the wheel, you can develop different types of color schemes that serve specific purposes.

Analogous Color Schemes

Analogous colors are adjacent to each other on the color wheel.

Adjacent colors highlighted (Cyan, Blue, Blue-Violet)

Effect: Creates cohesive and soothing experiences

Complementary Color Schemes

Complementary colors are located on opposite ends of the color wheel, relative to each other.

Opposite colors highlighted (Blue & Yellow)

Another Example:

Magenta & Green

Effect: Creates high contrast and visual impact

Color Contrast & Accessibility

Color contrast is essential for web accessibility. It ensures that text and other important elements are clearly distinguishable from their background.

Why It Matters: This is especially important for people with visual disabilities.

Good Contrast

Black on White

Poor Contrast

White on Yellow

Triadic Color Schemes

Triadic colors are approximately equidistant from each other on the color wheel. When connected, they form an equilateral triangle.

Equidistant colors forming a triangle (Red, Green, Blue)

Effect: Creates vibrant and dynamic color combinations

Monochromatic Color Schemes

In a monochromatic color scheme, all colors are derived from the same base color by adjusting its lightness, darkness, and saturation.

Single hue (Blue) with variations in lightness and saturation

Effect: Evokes a feeling of unity and harmony while still creating contrast

Tips for Using Color in Web Development

  • Create a color scheme that defines your website's branding
  • Use colors to evoke the emotions and perceptions that align with your goals
  • Choose colors with enough contrast to make your website accessible for everyone
  • Use color to highlight important elements of your website, like buttons
  • Use color consistently and use it to create a visual hierarchy

Key Takeaways

Color theory is a core aspect of design. By understanding color psychology, harmonies, and accessibility, you can:

  • Create effective designs
  • Evoke specific emotions
  • Enhance user experience
