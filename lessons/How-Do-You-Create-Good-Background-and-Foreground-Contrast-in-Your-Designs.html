Background & Foreground Contrast

Creating good contrast between background and foreground elements is essential for accessibility and usability.

What Is Contrast?

Contrast is the difference between two colors - or how easy it is to tell them apart.

High vs Low Contrast

Colors with higher contrast will be more visually distinct, whereas colors with lower contrast will be more visually similar.

Examples

  • High Contrast: Black and white have a very high contrast ratio
  • Low Contrast: Light blue and light purple have less contrast

What Is "Good Enough" Contrast?

You can't base it solely on how the text looks to you, as every user will have a different experience.

The Solution

Thankfully, the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) provide a standard for this.

WCAG Contrast Standards

AA Standard (Minimum)

4.5:1 contrast ratio

The bare minimum you should follow to be accessible to most users.

AAA Standard (Enhanced)

7:1 contrast ratio

Ensures the best level of accessibility.

How to Check Contrast Ratios

Available Tools

  • Websites: There are a number of websites that can check the contrast ratio between two colors
  • Browser DevTools: Most browsers will allow you to do this directly in the developer tools on your website

These tools calculate the exact contrast ratio and tell you if it meets WCAG standards.

How can you fix this?

Three Aspects That Impact Contrast

Hue

The general color type

(red, blue, orange)

Saturation

The "amount" of color present

(intensity of the color)

Lightness

How much white is present

(brightness of the color)

Step 1: Change the Hue

The first approach is to shift to a hue that is further away from blue. Let's use red.

After: Red on Blue (1.49:1)

Unfortunately, this change only brought the contrast ratio to 1.49:1. Still not enough!

Step 2: Change the Saturation

Next, you need to change the saturation, or the "amount" of color present. Let's increase the amount of red in the text.

After: More Saturated Red (3.54:1)

That brings us much closer to the goal, with a 3.54:1 contrast ratio. But we can do better!

Step 3: Change the Lightness

You can potentially get even closer by changing the lightness, which represents how much white is present in the color.

Let's decrease the lightness of the red to make it darker.

After: Much Darker Red (10.34:1) ✓

Much darker red text on light blue

Now there is a much darker red against the light blue background, which brings the contrast ratio to 10.34:1 - exceeding AAA!

Finding the Right Balance

You could continue to adjust the colors to find the balance between:

Two Competing Interests

  • The visual effect you want
  • An accessible contrast ratio

But it is important to remember that accessibility should always take precedence.

Key Takeaways

Remember These Points

  • Contrast is the difference between colors and affects readability
  • WCAG Standards: AA (4.5:1) minimum, AAA (7:1) enhanced
  • Use tools to check contrast ratios - don't rely on visual judgment
  • Three aspects impact contrast: hue, saturation, lightness
  • Accessibility first - always prioritize accessible contrast ratios

Design Accessible Experiences

By understanding and applying proper contrast ratios, you create designs that are accessible to everyone.

Best Practices

  • Test contrast ratios during design, not after
  • Use browser DevTools or online checkers
  • Aim for AAA standards when possible
  • Never sacrifice accessibility for aesthetics

Good contrast improves usability for all users, not just those with visual impairments!
