Section 1 — Design Heuristics

Jakob's Law: Users Expect Conventions

Users spend most of their time on other websites. They expect yours to work like those.

  • Shopping cart → top-right corner
  • Logo → links to homepage
  • Hamburger ↔ toggles navigation
  • Back arrow → goes back
When to break a convention: Only when the payoff (clarity, brand differentiation) clearly outweighs the cost (confusion, learning curve). Every broken convention adds cognitive load.
Snapchat lesson: Counter-conventional interface caused user drop-off. They eventually added a conventional UI layer. Innovate in content and value, not in where the shopping cart goes.

Common conventions: logo top-left, cart top-right, search in header. Move them and users get confused.

Fitts' Law: Size & Distance Matter

Time to reach a target depends on its size and distance. Larger + closer = faster.

Interactive: Click the Targets

Try clicking each. Notice which is easiest to hit.

X
OK
Go!

Click a target to see the effect of size.

  • Mobile tap targets: min 44×44px (Apple HIG) or 48×48dp (Google)
  • Destructive buttons (Delete, Remove): small + far away = intentional safety mechanism
  • macOS dock: icons expand on hover = adaptive Fitts' in action

Small + far = slow. Large + close = fast. Primary buttons should be the easiest to hit.

Section 1 — Design Heuristics

Affordances & Signifiers

An affordance is a visual cue that tells users what an element does before they interact with it.

Can You Tell What Each Does?

Button → affords clicking (raised, colored, labeled)
Learn more Link → affords clicking (underlined, colored text)
⠇ Drag handle → affords dragging (six-dot grid)
🗑 Icon alone → ambiguous. Delete? Clear? Discard?
🗑 Delete Icon + label = affordance + signifier — always better

Each element's visual style communicates what it does before interaction.

The 10-Second Test

Show your page for 10 seconds, then ask:

  1. "What is this page for?"
  2. "What should you do next?"

If they can't answer both, the hierarchy and affordances failed.

Signifiers: Labels and icons that make affordances explicit. An icon without a label works for experts but fails beginners. When in doubt, add the label.
Section 1 — Design Heuristics

The Design Critique Framework

Five questions to evaluate any design. Ground feedback in principles, not preferences.

1 What is the hierarchy?

What draws the eye first, second, third? Squint at the page. If the most important element doesn't stand out, the hierarchy is broken.

2 What is the primary action?

Is there one obvious CTA, or is the user's attention split between competing buttons? A great page has a single dominant action visually distinct from everything else.

3 What is the cognitive load?

How many decisions must the user make in the first 5 seconds? If it's more than 5–7 options, users feel overwhelmed. Apply Miller's Law and progressive disclosure.

4 Does it follow conventions?

What patterns from other sites are used or broken? Jakob's Law says users expect conventions. If conventions are broken, is the payoff worth the confusion cost?

5 What would you change?

Propose one specific improvement with a reason grounded in a design principle. "Make the CTA larger and add more whitespace" is good. "Make it pop more" is not.

Preference vs. Critique:
❌ "I don't like blue" = preference (subjective, not actionable).
✅ "The blue CTA doesn't contrast enough with the blue header" = critique (specific, principle-based, fixable).
Section 2 — UX Fundamentals

User-Centered Design

Visual design makes things look right. UX design makes things work right. The best interfaces do both.

The UCD Loop

Research
Understand users
→
Design
Prototype solutions
→
Test
Watch real users
→
Iterate
Improve & repeat
  • Research: Talk to real users. Even 5 interviews reveal patterns.
  • Design: Start with wireframes, not pixel-perfect mockups. Generate options first.
  • Test: Put prototypes in front of real people and stay silent. Their struggle IS the data.
  • Iterate: Fix what you learned, then test again. Most products need 2–3 cycles minimum.
You are not the user. Your assumptions about what is "obvious" are wrong until tested. This is the curse of knowledge — once you know how your interface works, you can't un-know it. The only antidote is watching someone else try to use it.
Google's homepage: Minimal not by philosophy, but because Larry Page didn't know enough HTML to make it complex. One input, one button, zero cognitive load. Design by constraint became design by intent. Simplicity almost always wins.
The #1 UCD mistake: Skipping Research and Test. Developers jump straight to Build because it feels productive. But building the wrong thing fast is worse than building the right thing slow.
Section 2 — UX Fundamentals

Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics

Published in 1994 by Jakob Nielsen. Still the industry standard 30 years later. Use these as a checklist when evaluating any interface.

1System StatusProgress bars, spinners, "Saving..." indicators. Never leave users wondering if anything happened.
2Real-World MatchUser language, not jargon. "Check in" not "Reservation start date." "Shopping cart" not "Order queue."
3User Control & FreedomUndo, cancel, back. Gmail's "Undo Send" — a 30-second grace period that prevents regret.
4ConsistencySame words, same actions, everywhere. If "Save" is blue on one page, it's blue on every page.
5Error PreventionDate pickers > free-text date fields. Disable submit until required fields are filled.
6Recognition > RecallRecently viewed items, autofill, breadcrumbs. Don't make users remember what they did before.
7FlexibilityKeyboard shortcuts for experts (Gmail j/k) that don't burden beginners who click buttons.
8Minimalist DesignEvery extra element competes with relevant info. Dialogues should contain nothing irrelevant.
9Error Recovery"Password must be 8+ characters" not "Error: invalid input." Plain language + fix suggestion.
10Help & DocsTooltips, inline hints, searchable help centers. Even obvious systems sometimes need documentation.

Section 2 — UX Fundamentals

UX Patterns Every Designer Should Know

Every app has these moments. Great designers plan for them. Bad designers forget they exist. Silence after action = anxiety. Always confirm that something happened.

Empty State

Friendly illustration + clear CTA to get started. Never leave a blank screen.

Loading (Skeleton Screen)

Gray placeholders mimic the layout. Feels faster than a spinner.

Error State

GitHub's friendly 404: personality + clear next step.

Error Messages

Good: says what went wrong + how to fix. Bad: "Error: invalid input."

Section 3 — Usability Testing

You Are Not the User

Once you know how your interface works, you can't un-know it. That's the "curse of knowledge." The only way to find real problems: watch someone else try to use it.

The 5-User Rule (Jakob Nielsen)

Testing with just 5 users reveals ~85% of usability problems. You don't need 50 participants or a lab.

Steep climb through 5 users, then diminishing returns. Test early, test often, test cheap.

The first iPhone had no copy-paste for 2 years. Apple's designers debated how to implement it. Users just wanted to copy a phone number. User testing drives features — not internal debates.

Think-Aloud Protocol

The most common testing method. Ask users to narrate their thoughts while using your site:

"I'm looking for the login... I think it's up here... Oh, it says 'Get Started' — is that the same thing?"

A real think-aloud session. The tester stays silent and watches.

What to Watch For

  • Hesitation = confusion (something isn't clear)
  • Backtracking = lost (navigation failed)
  • Misreading labels = naming problem
  • Skipping elements = invisible affordance
Critical rule: The tester stays silent. No hints, no "try clicking there." If they're stuck, that IS the finding.
Section 3 — Usability Testing

Testing Methods

Different methods answer different questions. Use the right one for the right situation.

Task-Based Testing

Don't ask "What do you think of my site?" Give real tasks:

  • "Find the return policy"
  • "Add this item to your cart and check out"
  • "Book a flight from SLC to Denver for next Friday"

Measure: completion rate, time on task, number of errors, satisfaction rating

Guerrilla Testing

Quick, informal, free. Grab a friend, classmate, or anyone who hasn't seen your design.

  • Show for 10 seconds (the 10-second test)
  • Ask: "What is this?" and "What would you click?"
  • Best for catching obvious problems early

Not a replacement for structured testing, but infinitely better than no testing.

A/B Testing

Show version A to half your users, version B to the other half. Measure which performs better.

Famous example: Google tested 41 shades of blue for a link color. The winning shade generated $200 million more in annual revenue. Small details, massive impact.

Limitation: A/B testing tells you WHAT works, not WHY. Pair with qualitative testing (think-aloud) for the full picture.
Usability Red Flags: High bounce rate on CTA pages, form abandonment, rage clicks (rapid clicking on non-interactive elements), back-button bouncing.
Section 3 — Usability Testing

Heatmaps & Analytics

Tools like Hotjar and Microsoft Clarity record where users click, scroll, and hover. They turn raw data into visual patterns.

Real Heatmap Example

Bright spots = high engagement. Cold spots = ignored areas. Users focus on headlines and CTAs.

Session recordings let you watch real users navigate your site. Rage clicks (rapid clicking on non-clickable elements) reveal frustration you'd never find by looking at your own design.

Scroll Depth: The Hidden Problem

Most users never scroll past 50% of a long page. If your CTA is at the bottom, most users never see it.

100% see this Hero
~80% reach here Features
~50% reach here Testimonials
~30% reach here Pricing
~15% reach here Footer CTA
The lesson: Put your most important CTA above the fold. Repeat it at strategic scroll depths if needed.

Google Analytics Signals

  • Bounce rate: % of visitors who leave without interacting
  • Exit pages: Where users abandon your site
  • Conversion funnels: Where users drop off in a multi-step process

Dark Patterns: What to Never Build

Now that you know what good UX looks like, here's the opposite. These are deceptive design patterns that trick users into doing things they didn't intend.

Confirm-Shaming

Making the "no" option guilt-inducing so users feel bad about declining:

Want 20% off?
Yes, save me money! No thanks, I prefer paying full price

More examples: "No, I don't want to grow my business" • "I'll pass on free shipping" • "No thanks, I hate saving money"

Forced Continuity

Free trial silently auto-converts to paid. No warning email. Cancel button buried 5 clicks deep.

Real examples: Many services require a phone call or certified letter to cancel a digital subscription.

Roach Motel

Easy to sign up, nearly impossible to cancel. Amazon Prime's cancellation flow was 6+ pages before the EU forced a 2-click process.

Misdirection

Using visual hierarchy to guide users toward the company's preferred option:

Accept All Cookies manage preferences

Cookie consent dialogs are the most common example.

Trick Questions

Double negatives and confusing checkboxes designed to trip you up:

☐ "Uncheck this box if you'd prefer not to not receive emails"
☑ "I do not wish to opt out of receiving promotional offers"
Hidden Unsubscribe / Sneaking

Opt-outs buried in tiny gray text, or items added to your cart without consent. GoDaddy pre-checks "domain privacy" ($10/yr) during checkout.

The bottom line: Dark patterns destroy trust and increasingly violate consumer protection laws. In 2023, Epic Games paid $245M in FTC fines partly for dark patterns in Fortnite. Design ethically.
Section 4 — Mobile-First Design

Mobile-First: A Design Philosophy

Mobile-first is not a CSS technique — it's a way of thinking. Start with the smallest screen and only add complexity as space allows.

Start with mobile (constraints force clarity), then expand for larger screens.

Three Questions at Every Stage

  1. What is the single most important thing on this page?
  2. What can be removed without losing the page's purpose?
  3. What can move behind a tap, toggle, or "See more" link?
Desktop-first hides bad prioritization. Mobile-first reveals it. 320px means every element must earn its place.

Examples You Know

  • Twitter/X: One text input — "What's happening?" Mobile constraint forced that clarity. Desktop inherited it.
  • TikTok: One video fills the entire screen. Swipe for the next. Zero navigation clutter.
  • Instagram: Photo feed, one column, thumb-friendly bottom navigation. Desktop is basically the mobile app stretched wider.

Luke Wroblewski coined "Mobile First" in 2009 — the idea that designing for mobile first leads to better experiences on all devices.

The test: "What happens at phone / tablet / 27″ monitor?" If you can't answer all three, you don't have a responsive design — you have a desktop design that shrinks.
Section 4 — Mobile-First Design

Four Responsive Layout Patterns

You already know @media queries. Now learn the design vocabulary for what your layouts should do at each breakpoint.

1. Mostly Fluid

Multi-column layout stacks to single column on small screens. The most common pattern.

2. Column Drop

Columns drop one by one as the screen narrows: 3 → 2+1 → 1+1+1.

3. Layout Shifter

Layout changes dramatically between breakpoints. Nav moves from sidebar to top to hamburger. Most flexible but hardest to maintain.

4. Off Canvas

Content slides in/out from the side. Nav panels, filter drawers, settings trays. Used heavily in mobile apps.

Which should you use? Start with Mostly Fluid — it handles 90% of cases. Only reach for Layout Shifter or Off Canvas when the content truly demands it.
Bonus: Tiny Tweaks — Keep the same layout, just adjust font sizes, spacing, and image sizes at each breakpoint. Simplest approach for content-focused pages.
Section 5 — Wireframing

Wireframing: Plan Before You Build

A wireframe is a blueprint. You wouldn't build a house without one. Wireframing forces design decisions before you write a single line of code — when changes are cheap.

Why Wireframe First?

  • Moving a box on paper: 5 seconds
  • Moving a styled component in code: 1 hour+
  • Forces decisions about what goes on the page and where, before worrying about how it looks
  • The tool doesn't matter — pen and paper is fastest

A wireframe: just gray boxes, lines, and labels. No colors. No polish. Pure structure.

Low-Fi vs High-Fi

Low-Fi (Wireframe)

Gray boxes, "Lorem ipsum," no styling. Fast to create (5 min). Easy to throw away. Best for: exploring options, getting early feedback.

High-Fi (Mockup)

Real colors, images, interactions. Looks like the final product. Best for: user testing, stakeholder approval, developer handoff.

The #1 mistake: Jumping straight to high-fi. You fall in love with the visual details and resist changing the layout — even when the layout is wrong. Always start low-fi.
Section 5 — Wireframing

What Every Wireframe Should Address

Before opening any design tool, answer these five questions. They force you to think about structure and priority before pixels.

1 Content hierarchy

What is the most important thing on this page? That goes at the top and gets the most visual weight. If you can't rank your content, you don't understand your page's purpose yet.

2 Navigation placement

Top bar, sidebar, or hamburger? For most sites, a top horizontal nav is the convention (Jakob's Law). Sidebars work for dashboards. Hamburger menus save space but hide options.

3 CTA placement

Where is the primary action button? Is it the most visually prominent element? A wireframe should have one obvious CTA a user can identify within 3 seconds.

4 Content blocks & order

Hero, features, testimonials, pricing, footer — what order tells the best story? Mirror the user's decision process: "What is this?" → "Why should I care?" → "What do I do next?"

5 Responsive plan

Sketch both mobile AND desktop. How does your 3-column feature grid behave on a phone? A wireframe without a responsive plan is only half a wireframe.

The journey: rough sketch → refined wireframe → high-fi mockup → final coded site.

Section 5 — Wireframing

The 5-Step Wireframing Process

Follow these steps for your assignment. Steps 1–4 should take no more than 20 minutes. The most common mistake? Skipping them entirely.

1 List the content your page needs

Bullet points, not design. "Hero heading, subhead, 3 feature cards, testimonial, footer." Write it as a list first.

2 Rank by importance

What must the user see first? That's your hero. What's second? This IS your visual hierarchy.

3 Sketch 2–3 rough layouts on paper

5 minutes each. No perfectionism. Just rectangles and labels. Try different arrangements before committing.

4 Pick the strongest layout

Which one serves the hierarchy best? Which passes the 10-second test? Which makes the primary action obvious? Choose one and refine it.

5 Move to a high-fi tool

NOW — and only now — open a design tool (Google Stitch, Figma, etc.) to bring your layout to life with real colors, images, and text.

For your assignment: Sketch wireframes FIRST, then use Google Stitch to generate high-fidelity mockups from your layout decisions. The wireframe is the thinking. The tool is the execution.
Remember: Pen and paper is fastest. Balsamiq, Figma, even PowerPoint all work. The tool doesn't matter — the thinking does.

Q&A

Questions & Answers

Q: What's the difference between UI and UX design?
UI = how it looks (colors, typography, layout, visual hierarchy). UX = how it works (task flows, error handling, navigation, usability). Both matter. A beautiful interface that confuses users is a failure.
Q: Do I really need to test with users?
Yes. Heuristics prevent common mistakes but can't predict real user behavior. 5 users + 30 minutes reveals problems no internal review catches. The first iPhone had no copy-paste for two years because internal debates replaced user testing.
Q: Is it ever OK to break Jakob's Law?
Yes, but only when the benefit clearly outweighs the cost. Every broken convention adds cognitive load. Innovate in your content and value proposition, not in where you put the shopping cart icon. Snapchat broke convention and paid for it in user drop-off.
Q: What are dark patterns?
Deceptive techniques that trick users: confirm-shaming, hidden unsubscribe, forced continuity, misdirection. They destroy trust, generate complaints, and increasingly violate consumer protection laws. Epic Games paid $245M in FTC fines partly for dark patterns.
Q: Why design mobile-first instead of desktop-first?
Mobile forces prioritization. 320px means you must decide what's essential. Desktop always has room to add "one more thing" and hide bad prioritization. Mobile-first forces you to be ruthless, and that clarity benefits all screen sizes.
Q: What's the difference between a wireframe and a mockup?
Wireframe = low-fi: gray boxes, placeholder text, no colors. Focuses on layout and structure. Mockup = high-fi: real colors, images, interactions. Looks like the final product. Always start with wireframes — jumping to high-fi makes you fall in love with the details and resist changing the layout.
Q: What is the think-aloud protocol and why is the tester supposed to stay silent?
Think-aloud asks users to narrate their thoughts while completing tasks. The tester stays silent because any hint (even "try clicking there") invalidates the finding. Their struggle IS the data. Helping them solves the symptom but hides the disease.
Q: What responsive layout pattern should I use for my first project?
Mostly Fluid. It handles 90% of use cases — multi-column on desktop, stacked on mobile. Only reach for Layout Shifter or Off Canvas when the content truly demands a different approach. Complexity is a cost; pay it only when you must.
Q: How do I know if my affordances are clear?
The 10-second test: show your page for 10 seconds and ask "What is this for?" and "What do you do next?" If users can't answer, your affordances failed. Also watch for rage clicks in heatmap recordings — repeated clicking on a non-interactive element means users expect it to respond.
Q: What's the most important of Nielsen's 10 heuristics?
Arguably #1 (Visibility of System Status) — users should always know what's happening. Silence after an action (no confirmation, no feedback) creates anxiety. But in practice, heuristics #3 (User Control) and #9 (Error Recovery) are the most commonly violated and the most visible to users when broken.
