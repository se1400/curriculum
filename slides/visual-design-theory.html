Section 1 — Introduction

Why Design Thinking Matters

Design is not about taste. It is about communication: guiding users to the right content with minimal friction.

  • Users scan, they don't read — you get 5–10 seconds
  • They ask: "What is this?" and "What should I do?"
  • 75% of users judge credibility by visual design (Stanford study)
  • Every CSS decision is a design decision
The hard truth: The back button is always one click away. If your design can't answer both questions in 10 seconds, the user leaves.

Same function. Wildly different design. Both list rentals — one communicates trust and clarity from the first second.

Section 2 — Visual Hierarchy

The F-Pattern

On content-heavy pages (blogs, news, search results), eye-tracking studies show users read in an F shape:

  1. Read the top line fully (horizontal bar)
  2. Scan down the left edge (vertical stripe)
  3. Occasionally dart rightward (shorter bars)
Design implication: Put your most important content on the left edge. Headlines, subheadings, and bullet points get the most attention.

Best for: Articles, documentation, search results, email inboxes

Section 2 — Visual Hierarchy

The Z-Pattern

On sparse, image-driven pages (landing pages, hero sections), the eye traces a Z shape:

  1. Top-left: Logo / brand
  2. Top-right: Navigation / secondary action
  3. Diagonal: Across the page
  4. Bottom-right: Primary CTA ← this is where the eye finishes
Key takeaway: Place your primary CTA at the terminal position of the Z (bottom-right). That's where the eye naturally lands.

Best for: Landing pages, hero sections, marketing pages

The Z-pattern traces: logo → nav → diagonal → CTA button

Section 2 — Visual Hierarchy

Whitespace Is Not Empty Space

Whitespace is a deliberate design choice that communicates confidence and premium quality.

  • Crowded pages feel chaotic
  • Spacious pages feel trustworthy
  • Isolation makes elements feel important
  • When in doubt, add more space
Apple's approach: A single product photo floating in white, with a short headline. The whitespace is the message — premium, confident, nothing to prove.

Section 3 — Gestalt Principles

How the Brain Groups Things

German psychologists discovered: we perceive groups and patterns before individual elements. Design with the brain's wiring, not against it.

ProximityClose = related
SimilarityLook alike = same type
ContinuityEye follows lines
ClosureBrain completes shapes
Figure/GroundForeground vs background
Common FateMove together = grouped
SymmetrySymmetry = calm; asymmetry = energy
The practical rule: If elements belong together, make them look together. If they don't, visually separate them.

Miller's Law: The 7 ± 2 Rule

Short-term memory holds roughly 7 ± 2 items. Chunking breaks information into digestible groups.

Section 4 — Cognitive Load

Hick's Law: Fewer Options, Faster Decisions

The more options a user has, the longer it takes to decide. The relationship is logarithmic.

50-button remote vs. 5-button remote. Same TV. Different experience.

Real-World Examples

  • Netflix: 3 plans, not 10. Decide in seconds.
  • E-commerce: Progressive filtering, not all 847 products at once
  • Onboarding: One question per screen
Caution: Fewer is not always better. Removing useful options creates frustration. Eliminate unnecessary choices, not functionality.

Section 5 — Color & Typography

The 60-30-10 Rule

Allocate your color palette in this ratio:

  • 60% dominant: Neutral background (white, light gray)
  • 30% secondary: Cards, sidebars, text blocks
  • 10% accent: CTAs, links, alerts — stands out because it's rare

Section 6 — Design Heuristics

Jakob's Law: Users Expect Conventions

Users spend most of their time on other websites. They expect yours to work like those.

  • Shopping cart → top-right corner
  • Logo → links to homepage
  • Hamburger ↔ toggles navigation
  • Back arrow → goes back
Snapchat lesson: Counter-conventional interface caused user drop-off. They eventually added a conventional UI layer. Break conventions only when the payoff clearly outweighs the confusion.

Common conventions: logo top-left, cart top-right, search in header. Move them and users get confused.

Fitts' Law: Size & Distance Matter

Time to reach a target depends on its size and distance. Larger + closer = faster.

Affordances & Signifiers

An affordance is a visual cue that tells users what an element does.

Can You Tell What Each Does?

Button → affords clicking (raised, colored, labeled)
Learn more Link → affords clicking (underlined, colored text)
⠇ Drag handle → affords dragging (six-dot grid)
🗑 Icon alone → ambiguous. Delete? Clear? Discard?
🗑 Delete Icon + label = affordance + signifier — always better

Each element's visual style communicates what it does before interaction.

The 10-Second Test

Show your page for 10 seconds, then ask:

  1. "What is this page for?"
  2. "What should you do next?"

If they can't answer both, the hierarchy failed.

Section 6 — Design Heuristics

The Design Critique Framework

Five questions to evaluate any design. Ground feedback in principles, not preferences.

1 What is the hierarchy?

What draws the eye first, second, third?

2 What is the primary action?

One obvious CTA, or attention split?

3 What is the cognitive load?

How many decisions in the first 5 seconds?

4 Does it follow conventions?

What patterns are used or broken?

5 What would you change?

One specific improvement with a reason.

Example: "I don't like blue" = preference. "The blue CTA doesn't contrast enough with the blue header, reducing its visual weight" = critique.

Key States to Handle

  • Loading: Skeleton screens feel faster than spinners (Facebook/LinkedIn)

Section 8 — Usability Testing

You Are Not the User

Once you know how your interface works, you can't un-know it. The only way to find real problems: watch someone else try.

The 5-User Rule

Testing with 5 users reveals ~85% of usability problems. You don't need 50.

Steep climb through 5 users, then diminishing returns.

Think-Aloud Protocol

Ask users to narrate: "I'm looking for the login... I think it's up here..."

What to watch for:

  • Hesitation = confusion
  • Backtracking = lost
  • Misreading labels = naming problem
  • Skipping elements = invisible affordance
Critical rule: Stay silent. No hints. If they're stuck, that is the finding.
Section 8 — Usability Testing

Testing Methods

Task-Based Testing

Don't ask "What do you think?" Give specific tasks:

  • "Find the return policy"
  • "Book a flight SLC → Denver"

Measure: completion rate, time, errors

Guerrilla Testing

Quick, informal, low-cost. Grab anyone who hasn't seen your design.

  • Show for 10 seconds
  • Ask them to complete a task
  • Best for catching obvious problems early

A/B Testing

Version A → 50% of users. Version B → other 50%. Measure.

Google tested 41 shades of blue. Winner = $200M/year more revenue.

Heatmaps & Analytics: Tools like Hotjar and Microsoft Clarity show where users click, scroll, and hover. Rage clicks = frustration.
Red flags: High bounce rate on CTA pages, form abandonment, rage clicks, back-button bouncing.
Section 8 — Usability Testing

Dark Patterns: What to Never Build

Now that you know what good UX looks like, here's what to never build:

Confirm-shaming

"No thanks, I don't want to save money."

Hidden Unsubscribe

Opt-out buried in tiny text behind multiple clicks.

Forced Continuity

Free trial auto-converts to paid with no warning.

Misdirection

Visual hierarchy highlights the company's preferred option while hiding yours.

These may boost short-term metrics but destroy trust, generate complaints, and increasingly violate consumer protection laws (EU Digital Services Act). Design ethically.
Section 9 — Mobile-First Design

Mobile-First: A Design Philosophy

Mobile-first is not a CSS technique. It's a design philosophy: start with the smallest screen and add complexity as space allows.

Three Questions at Every Stage

  1. What is the single most important thing on this page?
  2. What can be removed without losing the page's purpose?
  3. What can move behind a tap, toggle, or "See more" link?
Desktop-first hides bad prioritization — there's always room for "one more thing." Mobile-first reveals it immediately.

Luke Wroblewski coined "Mobile First" in 2009. Twitter proved it: one input, "What's happening?" Mobile constraint forced clarity.

Four Responsive Layout Patterns

PatternBehavior
Mostly FluidColumns stack to single column. Most common.
Column DropColumns drop one at a time: 3 → 2+1 → 1+1+1
Layout ShifterLayout fundamentally reorganizes. Most flexible.
Off CanvasContent slides in/out from the side on demand.

Test: "What happens at phone / tablet / 27″ monitor?" If you can't answer all three, it's not responsive.

Section 10 — Wireframing

Wireframing: Plan Before You Build

A wireframe is a blueprint. No colors, no images, no final copy. Just boxes and labels showing what goes where.

Why Wireframe?

  • Moving a box on paper: 5 seconds
  • Moving a styled section in code: 1 hour
  • Forces decisions about what and where before how it looks

Intentionally rough. Gray boxes, labels, no style to debate.

Low-Fi vs High-Fi

Low-Fi

Gray boxes, placeholders. Fast to create. Easy to throw away.

High-Fi

Real colors, images, text. Looks final. Takes much longer.

The mistake: Jumping straight to high-fi. You fall in love with visuals and resist changing the layout — even when it's wrong.

Section 10 — Wireframing

The 5-Step Wireframing Process

1 List the content

Bullet points, not design. What info must be on this page?

2 Rank by importance

What must users see first? This becomes your hierarchy.

3 Sketch 2–3 layouts

5 min each. No perfectionism. Rectangles + labels.

4 Pick the strongest

Which serves the hierarchy best? Passes the 10-second test?

5 Move to high-fi tool

Now — and only now — open a design tool.

Steps 1–4 should take no more than 20 minutes. The most common mistake is skipping them and opening the design tool first. Start with paper. Always.

Q&A

Questions & Answers

Q: Do I need to be artistic to be good at design?
No. Design is problem-solving, not art. Rough rectangles and clear thinking about content priority is all you need.
Q: What's the difference between UI and UX design?
UI = how it looks (colors, typography, layout). UX = how it works (task flows, error handling, navigation). Best products nail both.
Q: How do I choose colors if I'm not a designer?
Start with 60-30-10. White (60%), dark gray (30%), blue accent (10%). Verify with WebAIM Contrast Checker. Try Coolors for palettes.
Q: Why do so many websites use blue?
Blue is the most universally liked color across cultures. It conveys trust and stability. Facebook, LinkedIn, PayPal all use it.
Q: What's the most common beginner design mistake?
Making everything look important. Hierarchy requires contrast between levels. Design is subtraction, not addition.
Q: Do I really need to test with users?
Yes. Heuristics prevent common mistakes but can't predict real user behavior. 5 users + 30 minutes reveals problems no internal review catches.
Q: Why design mobile-first instead of desktop-first?
Mobile forces prioritization. 320px means you must decide what's essential. Desktop lets you avoid that decision because there's always room.
Q: What are dark patterns?
Deceptive techniques that trick users: confirm-shaming, hidden unsubscribe, forced continuity. They destroy trust and increasingly violate consumer protection laws.
