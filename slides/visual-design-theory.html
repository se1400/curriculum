Section 1 — Introduction

Why Design Thinking Matters

Design is not about taste. It is about communication: guiding users to the right content with minimal friction.

  • Users scan, they don't read — you get 5–10 seconds
  • They ask: "What is this?" and "What should I do?"
  • 75% of users judge credibility by visual design (Stanford study)
  • Every CSS decision is a design decision
The hard truth: The back button is always one click away. If your design can't answer both questions in 10 seconds, the user leaves.

Same function. Wildly different design. Both list rentals — one communicates trust and clarity from the first second.

Section 2 — Visual Hierarchy

The F-Pattern

On content-heavy pages (blogs, news, search results), eye-tracking studies show users read in an F shape:

  1. Read the top line fully (horizontal bar)
  2. Scan down the left edge (vertical stripe)
  3. Occasionally dart rightward (shorter bars)
Design implication: Put your most important content on the left edge. Headlines, subheadings, and bullet points get the most attention.

Best for: Articles, documentation, search results, email inboxes

Section 2 — Visual Hierarchy

The Z-Pattern

On sparse, image-driven pages (landing pages, hero sections), the eye traces a Z shape:

  1. Top-left: Logo / brand
  2. Top-right: Navigation / secondary action
  3. Diagonal: Across the page
  4. Bottom-right: Primary CTA ← this is where the eye finishes
Key takeaway: Place your primary CTA at the terminal position of the Z (bottom-right). That's where the eye naturally lands.

Best for: Landing pages, hero sections, marketing pages

The Z-pattern traces: logo → nav → diagonal → CTA button

Section 3 — Gestalt Principles

How the Brain Groups Things

In the early 1900s, German psychologists discovered something powerful: our brains see groups and patterns first, individual elements second. Think about how you see a flock of birds as one shape, not 200 separate birds. These "Gestalt" principles explain how users automatically organize what they see on your webpage.

1. ProximityClose together = related
2. SimilarityLook alike = same type
3. ContinuityEye follows lines & curves
4. ClosureBrain completes shapes
5. Figure/GroundForeground vs background
6. Common FateMove together = grouped
7. Symmetry & OrderSymmetrical layouts feel calm and trustworthy; asymmetry feels dynamic and energetic
The golden rule: If elements belong together, make them look together. If they don't, visually separate them. When your layout feels "off" but you can't explain why, a Gestalt violation is almost always the cause.

All 7 principles visualized. Each one is a different way your brain automatically organizes visual information.

2. Similarity: Same Look = Same Type

Elements that look alike are perceived as the same kind of thing. Think of a sports team wearing matching jerseys — you instantly know who's on the same team, even in a crowd.

Navigation Bar Example

All nav links share the same style. The active one looks different to say "you are here."

Home About Products Contact

The yellow one is different → your brain immediately says "that's the active page."

Button Consistency

If buttons look the same, users expect them to behave the same:

⚠ The green button breaks similarity. Users pause — "Does this do something different?"

The rule: Same function = same style. Different function = different style. Never make things look the same if they do different things, and never make things look different if they do the same thing.

Social Media Example

On Instagram, every post card looks identical (square photo, username, heart/comment icons). This consistency tells you "these are all the same type of content." The one ad post that looks slightly different? You notice it immediately.

Section 3 — Gestalt Principles

4. Closure: Your Brain Fills in the Gaps

Your brain automatically completes incomplete shapes. You don't need to draw every line — the brain fills in what's missing. It's like reading a text with mssng vwls — you still understand it because your brain fills in the blanks.

The FedEx Hidden Arrow

The most famous example: look at the space between the E and the x in the FedEx logo. There's an arrow hidden there. Your brain uses closure to "see" the arrow shape even though it isn't explicitly drawn.

FedEx

Look between the E and x. See the arrow? That's closure at work — the arrow is created by the absence of shape, and your brain fills it in.

Progress Indicators

A partial circle — your brain sees the "complete" circle and understands 75% is filled.

In Web Design

Closure is everywhere in UI:

  • Loading rings — a partial circle implies "progress toward completion"
  • Card layouts — content cut off at the bottom implies "scroll for more"
  • Icon design — simple shapes suggest complex objects (a house icon = home, even though it's just a triangle + rectangle)
  • Hamburger menu — three horizontal lines suggest "there's a list of items hidden here"
Why it matters: You don't need to spell everything out. Users' brains will fill in the gaps — but only if you give them enough context. Too little information = confusion. Just enough = elegant simplicity.

5. Figure/Ground: What's in Front?

Your brain automatically separates what's in the foreground (the "figure" you focus on) from the background (the "ground" that recedes). Think of a spotlight on a stage — the performer is the figure, the dark background is the ground.

The dimmed page = "ground." The bright modal = "figure." Your brain instantly knows what to focus on.

6. Common Fate: Moving Together = Grouped

Elements that move in the same direction at the same time are perceived as one group. Think of a school of fish — they all turn together, so you see them as one unit, not hundreds of individuals.

Where You See Common Fate

  • Dropdown menus: All menu items slide out together → "these are one menu"
  • Accordions: When content expands, it all moves together → "this content belongs to this header"
  • Page scroll: Parallax effects where some elements move faster create a foreground/background separation
  • Notification toasts: A group of notifications that slide in together are perceived as related alerts
Analogy: Imagine a marching band — the trumpet section all steps in sync, the drum line moves differently. You can tell who belongs to which section by how they move, even without hearing them play.

Section 4 — Cognitive Load

Hick's Law: Fewer Options, Faster Decisions

The more options a user has, the longer it takes to decide. Think of a restaurant menu: 5 items — you pick fast. 200 items — you freeze.

50-button TV remote vs. 5-button Apple TV remote. Same TV. Wildly different experience.

Real-World Examples You Know

  • Netflix: Only 3 plan choices, not 10. You decide in seconds.
  • Instagram: One main feed, not 12 tabs competing for attention
  • Google: One search box. That's it. No menu of 20 search categories.
  • E-commerce: Use progressive filtering — start broad, then narrow down

Netflix shows exactly 3 options. Clear labels. One recommended. Decision time: seconds.

Caution: Fewer is not always better. Removing useful options creates a different kind of frustration. The goal: eliminate unnecessary choices, not useful functionality.
Think about it: Why does the iPhone home screen only show one page of apps at a time? Why does TikTok show one video at a time? Fewer simultaneous choices = faster engagement.
Section 4 — Cognitive Load

Progressive Disclosure

Show only what's needed right now. Reveal complexity when the user asks for it. Think of it like a book: you read one page at a time, not all 300 at once.

Gmail: The Perfect Example

Gmail's compose window. Simple by default. Click "Cc/Bcc" or formatting to reveal more options.

The pattern: Simple path is always visible. Advanced options are always available but hidden until needed. Beginners aren't overwhelmed. Experts aren't limited.

More Examples You've Seen

  • Instagram: Tap a photo to see likes & comments. They don't clutter the feed.
  • "See more" links: Show a summary, let users expand if they want depth
  • YouTube settings: Basic quality picker visible, advanced codec settings hidden behind "Advanced"
  • Google Forms: Basic settings visible, conditional logic behind a toggle

Sources of Cognitive Load to Eliminate

  • Jargon: Users see "SKU" or "session ID" — they shouldn't
  • Inconsistency: Blue buttons here, green buttons there — users pause to decode
  • Too many fonts: Each typeface demands its own mental decoding
  • Unlabeled icons: A trash icon alone is ambiguous. Icon + "Delete" = clear

Section 5 — Color & Typography

The 60-30-10 Rule

Think of decorating a room: 60% walls (neutral), 30% furniture (secondary), 10% pillows and art (accent). Same idea for websites.

The 60-30-10 rule keeps your color palette balanced and intentional.

How It Breaks Down

  • 60% dominant: Background & neutral space (white, light gray). This is the "air" your design breathes.
  • 30% secondary: Cards, sidebars, headers, text blocks. Supports the content.
  • 10% accent: CTAs, links, alerts, badges. Stands out because it's rare.

Sites That Nail This

  • Stripe: White (60%) + dark gray (30%) + purple accent (10%)
  • Apple: White (60%) + product images (30%) + blue "Buy" buttons (10%)
  • YouTube: White (60%) + gray/video thumbnails (30%) + red subscribe/notifications (10%)
Why it works: When your accent color only appears on 10% of the page, it instantly draws the eye. Use it on the ONE thing you want people to click.
Section 5 — Color & Typography

Contrast & Accessibility

Contrast ratio measures how easy text is to read against its background. Higher number = easier to read.

What the Numbers Mean

The ratio compares the brightness of your text to its background:

  • 1:1 = identical colors (invisible text)
  • 2:1 = barely visible. Squinting required.
  • 4.5:1 = WCAG minimum for normal text. Readable for most people.
  • 7:1 = WCAG AAA. Comfortable for everyone, including low vision users.
  • 21:1 = maximum contrast (black on white)
Why this matters: People read on phones outdoors in sunlight, on dim monitors, and as they age. 4.5:1 is the minimum, not the goal.

Section 6 — Design Heuristics

Jakob's Law: Users Expect Conventions

Users spend most of their time on other websites. They expect yours to work like those.

  • Shopping cart → top-right corner
  • Logo → links to homepage
  • Hamburger ↔ toggles navigation
  • Back arrow → goes back
Snapchat lesson: Counter-conventional interface caused user drop-off. They eventually added a conventional UI layer. Break conventions only when the payoff clearly outweighs the confusion.

Section 6 — Design Heuristics

The Design Critique Framework

Five questions to evaluate any design. Ground feedback in principles, not preferences.

1 What is the hierarchy?

What draws the eye first, second, third?

2 What is the primary action?

One obvious CTA, or attention split?

3 What is the cognitive load?

How many decisions in the first 5 seconds?

4 Does it follow conventions?

What patterns are used or broken?

5 What would you change?

One specific improvement with a reason.

Example: "I don't like blue" = preference. "The blue CTA doesn't contrast enough with the blue header, reducing its visual weight" = critique.

Section 7 — UX Fundamentals

Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics

Published in 1994 by Jakob Nielsen. Still the industry standard 30 years later. Use these as a checklist when evaluating any interface.

1System StatusKeep users informed. Progress bars, spinners, "Saving..." indicators. Bad: clicking "Submit" and seeing nothing.
2Real-World MatchUse user language, not internal jargon. "Check in" not "Reservation start date." Airbnb nails this.
3User Control & FreedomUsers make mistakes. Give them undo, cancel, back. Gmail's "Undo Send" = 30-second grace period.
4ConsistencyIf "Save" is blue on one page, it's blue on every page. Same words, same actions, everywhere.
5Error PreventionBetter than good error messages: prevent the problem. Date pickers > free-text date fields.
6Recognition > RecallShow recently viewed items, autofill suggestions, breadcrumbs. Amazon's "Recently Viewed" bar.
7FlexibilityShortcuts for experts (Gmail: j/k keys) without burdening beginners. Both audiences served.
8Minimalist DesignEvery extra element competes with relevant info. Dialogues shouldn't have irrelevant content.
9Error Recovery"Password must be 8+ characters" not "Error: invalid input." Plain language + how to fix it.
10Help & DocsTooltips, inline hints, searchable help centers. Even if the system should work without docs.

Section 8 — Usability Testing

You Are Not the User

Once you know how your interface works, you can't un-know it. That's called the "curse of knowledge." The only way to find real problems: watch someone else try to use it.

The 5-User Rule (Jakob Nielsen)

Testing with just 5 users reveals ~85% of usability problems. You don't need 50 participants or a lab.

Steep climb through 5 users, then diminishing returns. Test early, test often, test cheap.

The first iPhone had no copy-paste for 2 years. Apple's designers debated how to implement it. Users just wanted to copy a phone number from a text. User testing drives features — not internal debates.

Think-Aloud Protocol

The most common testing method. Ask users to narrate their thoughts while using your site:

"I'm looking for the login... I think it's up here... Oh, it says 'Get Started' — is that the same thing?"

A real think-aloud session. The tester stays silent and watches.

What to Watch For

  • Hesitation = confusion (something isn't clear)
  • Backtracking = lost (navigation failed)
  • Misreading labels = naming problem
  • Skipping elements = invisible affordance
Critical rule: The tester stays silent. No hints, no "try clicking there." If they're stuck, that IS the finding.
Section 8 — Usability Testing

Testing Methods

Different methods answer different questions. Use the right one for the right situation.

Task-Based Testing

Don't ask "What do you think of my site?" Give real tasks:

  • "Find the return policy"
  • "Add this item to your cart and check out"
  • "Book a flight from SLC to Denver for next Friday"

Measure: completion rate, time on task, number of errors, satisfaction rating

Guerrilla Testing

Quick, informal, free. Grab a friend, classmate, or anyone who hasn't seen your design.

  • Show for 10 seconds (the 10-second test)
  • Ask: "What is this?" and "What would you click?"
  • Best for catching obvious problems early

Not a replacement for structured testing, but infinitely better than no testing.

A/B Testing

Show version A to half your users, version B to the other half. Measure which performs better.

Famous example: Google tested 41 shades of blue for a link color. The winning shade generated $200 million more in annual revenue. Small details, massive impact.

Limitation: A/B testing tells you WHAT works, not WHY. Pair with qualitative testing (think-aloud) for the full picture.
Usability Red Flags: High bounce rate on CTA pages, form abandonment, rage clicks (rapid clicking on non-interactive elements), back-button bouncing.

Dark Patterns: What to Never Build

Now that you know what good UX looks like, here's the opposite. These are deceptive design patterns that trick users into doing things they didn't intend.

Confirm-Shaming

Making the "no" option guilt-inducing:

Want 20% off?
Yes, save me money! No thanks, I prefer paying full price
Forced Continuity

Free trial silently auto-converts to paid subscription. No warning email. Cancel button buried 5 clicks deep.

You've seen this on streaming services, fitness apps, and SaaS tools.

Roach Motel

Easy to sign up, nearly impossible to cancel. Amazon Prime was famously called out for this. They had to simplify their cancellation flow after regulatory pressure.

Hidden Unsubscribe

The opt-out is buried in tiny gray text at the bottom, behind multiple confirmation dialogs:

unsubscribe (hidden in the footer, same color as the background)
Misdirection

Using visual hierarchy to guide users to the company's preferred option. The "accept all cookies" button is bright blue. The "manage preferences" link is tiny gray text.

Trick Questions

Double negatives and confusing checkboxes: "Uncheck this box if you'd prefer not to not receive emails."

The bottom line: Dark patterns may boost short-term metrics, but they destroy trust, generate complaints, and increasingly violate consumer protection laws (EU Digital Services Act, FTC enforcement). Companies get sued over these. Design ethically.
Section 9 — Mobile-First Design

Mobile-First: A Design Philosophy

Mobile-first is not a CSS technique — it's a way of thinking. Start with the smallest screen and only add complexity as space allows.

Start with mobile (constraints force clarity), then expand for larger screens.

Three Questions at Every Stage

  1. What is the single most important thing on this page?
  2. What can be removed without losing the page's purpose?
  3. What can move behind a tap, toggle, or "See more" link?
Desktop-first hides bad prioritization. Mobile-first reveals it. If you only have 320px of width, every element must earn its place.

Examples You Know

  • Twitter/X: One text input — "What's happening?" Mobile constraint forced that clarity. Desktop inherited it.
  • TikTok: One video fills the entire screen. Swipe for the next. Zero navigation clutter.
  • Instagram: Photo feed, one column, thumb-friendly bottom navigation. Desktop is basically the mobile app stretched wider.

Luke Wroblewski coined "Mobile First" in 2009 — the idea that designing for mobile first leads to better experiences on all devices.

The test: "What happens at phone / tablet / 27″ monitor?" If you can't answer all three, you don't have a responsive design — you have a desktop design that shrinks.
Section 9 — Mobile-First Design

Four Responsive Layout Patterns

You already know @media queries. Now learn the design vocabulary for what your layouts should do at each breakpoint.

1. Mostly Fluid

Multi-column layout stacks to single column on small screens. The most common pattern.

2. Column Drop

Columns drop one by one as the screen narrows: 3 → 2+1 → 1+1+1.

3. Layout Shifter

Layout changes dramatically between breakpoints. Nav moves from sidebar to top to hamburger. Most flexible but hardest to maintain.

4. Off Canvas

Content slides in/out from the side. Nav panels, filter drawers, settings trays. Used heavily in mobile apps.

Which should you use? Start with Mostly Fluid — it handles 90% of cases. Only reach for Layout Shifter or Off Canvas when the content truly demands it.
Bonus: Tiny Tweaks — Keep the same layout, just adjust font sizes, spacing, and image sizes at each breakpoint. Simplest approach for content-focused pages.
Section 10 — Wireframing

Wireframing: Plan Before You Build

A wireframe is a blueprint. You wouldn't build a house without one. Wireframing forces design decisions before you write a single line of code — when changes are cheap.

Why Wireframe First?

  • Moving a box on paper: 5 seconds
  • Moving a styled component in code: 1 hour+
  • Forces decisions about what goes on the page and where, before worrying about how it looks
  • The tool doesn't matter — pen and paper is fastest

A wireframe: just gray boxes, lines, and labels. No colors. No polish. Pure structure.

Low-Fi vs High-Fi

Low-Fi (Wireframe)

Gray boxes, "Lorem ipsum," no styling. Fast to create (5 min). Easy to throw away. Best for: exploring options, getting early feedback.

High-Fi (Mockup)

Real colors, images, interactions. Looks like the final product. Best for: user testing, stakeholder approval, developer handoff.

The #1 mistake: Jumping straight to high-fi. You fall in love with the visual details and resist changing the layout — even when the layout is wrong. Always start low-fi.

Section 10 — Wireframing

The 5-Step Wireframing Process

Follow these steps for your assignment. Steps 1–4 should take no more than 20 minutes. The most common mistake? Skipping them entirely.

1 List the content your page needs

Bullet points, not design. "Hero heading, subhead, 3 feature cards, testimonial, footer." Write it as a list first.

2 Rank by importance

What must the user see first? That's your hero. What's second? That determines your content flow. This IS your visual hierarchy.

3 Sketch 2–3 rough layouts on paper

5 minutes each. No perfectionism. Just rectangles and labels. "What if the features are in a grid?" "What if they're stacked?" Try different arrangements.

4 Pick the strongest layout

Which one serves the hierarchy best? Which passes the 10-second test? Which makes the primary action obvious? Choose one and refine it.

5 Move to a high-fi tool

NOW — and only now — open a design tool (Google Stitch, Figma, etc.) to bring your layout to life with real colors, images, and text.

For your assignment: Sketch wireframes FIRST, then use Google Stitch to generate high-fidelity mockups from your layout decisions. The wireframe is the thinking. The tool is the execution.
Remember: Pen and paper is fastest. Balsamiq, Figma, even PowerPoint all work. The tool doesn't matter — the thinking does.

Q&A

Questions & Answers

Q: Do I need to be artistic to be good at design?
No. Design is problem-solving, not art. Rough rectangles and clear thinking about content priority is all you need.
Q: What's the difference between UI and UX design?
UI = how it looks (colors, typography, layout). UX = how it works (task flows, error handling, navigation). Best products nail both.
Q: How do I choose colors if I'm not a designer?
Start with 60-30-10. White (60%), dark gray (30%), blue accent (10%). Verify with WebAIM Contrast Checker. Try Coolors for palettes.
Q: Why do so many websites use blue?
Blue is the most universally liked color across cultures. It conveys trust and stability. Facebook, LinkedIn, PayPal all use it.
Q: What's the most common beginner design mistake?
Making everything look important. Hierarchy requires contrast between levels. Design is subtraction, not addition.
Q: Do I really need to test with users?
Yes. Heuristics prevent common mistakes but can't predict real user behavior. 5 users + 30 minutes reveals problems no internal review catches.
Q: Why design mobile-first instead of desktop-first?
Mobile forces prioritization. 320px means you must decide what's essential. Desktop lets you avoid that decision because there's always room.
Q: What are dark patterns?
Deceptive techniques that trick users: confirm-shaming, hidden unsubscribe, forced continuity. They destroy trust and increasingly violate consumer protection laws.
Q: Is it ever OK to break Jakob's Law?
Yes, but only when the benefit clearly outweighs the cost. Every broken convention adds cognitive load. Innovate in your content and value proposition, not in where you put the shopping cart icon.
Q: What's the difference between a wireframe and a mockup?
Wireframe = low-fi: gray boxes, placeholder text, no colors. Focuses on layout. Mockup = high-fi: real colors, images, interactions. Looks like the final product. Always start with wireframes.
Q: How many navigation items should a site have?
Miller's Law says 7±2. In practice, 5–7 top-level items is the sweet spot. If you need more, use dropdown sub-menus. Apple.com uses exactly 7.
Q: What's the 60-30-10 rule and how do I use it?
Allocate 60% of your design to a dominant neutral color (background), 30% to a secondary color (cards, sidebars), and 10% to an accent color (CTAs, links). The accent stands out because it's rare. Stripe and Apple both follow this rule closely.
